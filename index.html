Simplifying Radicals

Foundations: Radicals & Numbers › Concept Foundation
What this idea means
KnowPart 1 of 3Skill 01/34
Core Rule
\[\sqrt{ab}=\sqrt a\sqrt b,\quad \sqrt{k^2}=|k|\]

This is the main pattern you should recognize before solving. Do not memorize it alone; connect it to the operation being undone or the structure being simplified.

!
Common Mistake
Do not write \(\sqrt{a+b}=\sqrt a+\sqrt b\). That rule is false.
Detailed Explanation
  • A radical is simplified when there are no perfect-square factors left inside the square root.
  • The main move is to break the radicand into a perfect square times the leftover factor.
  • For variables, remember that \(\sqrt{x^2}=|x|\). In many school problems they assume variables are nonnegative, so it becomes \(x\).
  • A simplified radical keeps irrational parts exact; do not replace \(\sqrt2\) by a decimal unless approximation is requested.
How to Think About It
  • List useful perfect squares: \(1,4,9,16,25,36,49,64,81,100,121,144\).
  • Choose the largest perfect square factor to finish in one step.
  • Simplify coefficients outside and keep the remaining radical inside.
Before moving on

Ask: What is the structure? What operation is being used? What rule allows me to rewrite it without changing its value?

Finding Square Roots

Foundations: Radicals & Numbers › Concept Foundation
What this idea means
KnowPart 1 of 3Skill 02/34
Core Rule
\[\sqrt{a}=b \Longleftrightarrow b^2=a,\quad b\ge 0\]

This is the main pattern you should recognize before solving. Do not memorize it alone; connect it to the operation being undone or the structure being simplified.

!
Common Mistake
Do not confuse \(\sqrt{25}=5\) with solving \(x^2=25\), which gives \(x=\pm5\).
Detailed Explanation
  • The square root asks: what nonnegative number squared gives the radicand?
  • Perfect squares have exact whole-number square roots, like \(\sqrt{49}=7\).
  • Non-perfect squares stay exact as radicals or can be approximated between two perfect squares.
  • The symbol \(\sqrt{a}\) means the principal, nonnegative square root.
How to Think About It
  • Check if the number is a perfect square first.
  • For approximation, locate it between two nearby perfect squares.
  • For equations like \(x^2=25\), there are two solutions: \(x=\pm5\).
Before moving on

Rationalizing the Denominator

Foundations: Radicals & Numbers › Concept Foundation
What this idea means
KnowPart 1 of 3Skill 03/34
Core Rule
\[\frac{a}{\sqrt b}\cdot\frac{\sqrt b}{\sqrt b}=\frac{a\sqrt b}{b}\]

This is the main pattern you should recognize before solving. Do not memorize it alone; connect it to the operation being undone or the structure being simplified.

!
Common Mistake
Do not multiply only the denominator. Whatever you do to the denominator must also be done to the numerator.
Detailed Explanation
  • Rationalizing means removing radicals from the denominator.
  • You multiply by a form of 1, so the value of the fraction does not change.
  • For one radical term, multiply numerator and denominator by that radical.
  • For a binomial denominator, use the conjugate: change the sign between the two terms.
How to Think About It
  • Single radical: multiply by the same radical.
  • Binomial with radical: multiply by the conjugate.
  • Simplify the result completely after rationalizing.
Before moving on

Scientific Notation

Number Forms › Concept Foundation
What this idea means
KnowPart 1 of 3Skill 04/34
Core Rule
\[a\times10^n,\quad 1\le |a|<10,\quad n\in\mathbb Z\]

This is the main pattern you should recognize before solving. Do not memorize it alone; connect it to the operation being undone or the structure being simplified.

!
Common Mistake
Do not write \(35.2 imes10^4\). The first factor must be between 1 and 10, so use \(3.52 imes10^5\).
Detailed Explanation
  • Scientific notation writes very large or very small numbers compactly.
  • The coefficient must be at least 1 and less than 10 in absolute value.
  • The exponent tells how many places the decimal point moved.
  • Positive exponents represent large numbers; negative exponents represent small decimals.
How to Think About It
  • Move the decimal until only one nonzero digit is before it.
  • Moving left gives a positive exponent.
  • Moving right gives a negative exponent.

Simplifying Algebraic Expressions

!
Common Mistake
Do not combine unlike terms: \(3x+4\) is not \(7x\).

  • Simplifying means rewriting an expression in a cleaner equivalent form.

  • Expressions do not have equal signs, so you simplify them; equations have equal signs, so you solve them.
How to Think About It
  • Distribute before combining terms when parentheses exist.
  • Combine only like terms.
  • Keep signs attached to their terms.

Factoring Quadratic Polynomials Using GCF

Quadratics › Concept Foundation
What this idea means
KnowPart 1 of 3Skill 06/34
Core Rule
\[ax^2+bx = x(ax+b),\quad ab+ac=a(b+c)\]

This is the main pattern you should recognize before solving. Do not memorize it alone; connect it to the operation being undone or the structure being simplified.

!
Common Mistake
Do not forget to include every term inside the parentheses after factoring.
Detailed Explanation
  • GCF means greatest common factor: the largest factor shared by all terms.
  • Before using advanced factoring, always check for a GCF first.
  • Factoring reverses distribution: you pull out the common factor.
  • This makes expressions easier to simplify and equations easier to solve.
How to Think About It
  • Find the GCF of the numerical coefficients.
  • Find the lowest power of each common variable.
  • Divide each term by the GCF and place the result inside parentheses.
Before moving on

Ask: What is the structure? What operation is being used? What rule allows me to rewrite it without changing its value?

Solving Linear Equations in One Variable

Equations › Concept Foundation
What this idea means
KnowPart 1 of 3Skill 07/34
Core Rule
\[ax+b=c \Rightarrow ax=c-b \Rightarrow x=\frac{c-b}{a}\]

This is the main pattern you should recognize before solving. Do not memorize it alone; connect it to the operation being undone or the structure being simplified.

!
Common Mistake
Do not change only one side of the equation. Balance is the whole logic.
Detailed Explanation
  • A linear equation in one variable has one unknown and the variable power is 1.
  • Solving means finding the value that makes the equation true.
  • You keep the equation balanced by doing the same operation to both sides.
  • The goal is to isolate the variable.
How to Think About It
  • Clear parentheses using distribution.
  • Combine like terms on each side.
  • Move variable terms to one side and constants to the other.
  • Divide by the coefficient of the variable.

  • A literal equation contains several variables.

How to Think About It

  • Undo operations around that variable in reverse order.
  • If the variable appears in multiple terms, factor it out.
